Priors persist
through suppression.

You tell a language model that doctor means forest. It says ok. But does it really stop pulling toward hospital? A Stroop-style paradigm reveals what's happening underneath.

Han-yu Wang  ·  The University of Hong Kong  ·  ORCID

01  ·  The setup

You tell the model. It says ok.
But does it really believe you?

Suppose a glossary tells the model that in this document, doctor means forest. The model reads it, accepts the rule, answers your follow-up questions. It looks like it understood.

But does it stop pulling toward hospital — doctor's strongest lexical neighbor? Or is it answering through the rule while still being tugged by what the word usually means?

This question matters whenever a model is asked to respect a local definition over its built-in vocabulary — glossaries, system prompts, technical documentation, RAG-grounded answers. The answer turns out to be more interesting than yes or no.

Override is not replacement. It's competition.

02  ·  The test

A Stroop card for meaning.

Stroop (1935) asked you to name the ink color of color words. RED printed in green ink is hard — the word fights the task. Our paradigm runs the same trick on meaning. A conflict prompt remaps a familiar word (doctor) to a contextual target (forest). A neutral control replaces the familiar word with something semantically weak (class), holding everything else fixed. The gap reveals how much the prior leaked.

03  ·  The pattern

The leak follows the prior.

One item could be a fluke. But across 7,744 item × prompt × model observations, the relationship is clean: items whose distractor has a stronger lexical prior — where the model defaults more confidently to hospital over forest, or to whatever the word usually means — show larger Stroop interference. The slope survives controls for answer prior, frequency, tokenization, prompt wrapper, conflict family, model family, scale, and instruction tuning.

05  ·  Inside the model

Where does the leak live?

Behavior tells us the leak is real and scales with the prior. To find where it lives, we use activation patching. We run the model twice: once on the conflict prompt (the run that leaks), once on the neutral control (the run that doesn't). We copy a slice of internal state — one token's activations at one layer — from the neutral run into the conflict run, and measure how much of the leak goes away.

Scope. Mechanistic results in §05–§07 are established on five aligned 1B–2B models (Qwen2.5-1.5B base/IT, Gemma-2-2B base/IT, OLMo-1B), the antonym family, the game-rule wrapper, and single-token target/distractor items — the regime where activation patching is tractable. The behavioral panels in §03–§04 cover the full 11-model × 4-family × 4-wrapper grid.

06  ·  The triplet

Subject. Target. Query.
One binding.

The three positions don't recover the effect by simply adding up. They bind. Subject and query supply the identity match — which word is being redefined. Target supplies the local value — what it's being redefined to. Replace the target activation with a donor item's, keeping subject and query matched, and recovery collapses in every model. The triplet does binding, not retrieval.

MODEL

Drop the target: replace only the definition target activation with a donor item's, keeping subject and query matched. Recovery collapses in every model — the triplet is doing binding, not just identity matching.

The rule lives in a three-token signature in the residual stream. Not at one position. Not by simple addition. By a bound triplet.

07  ·  The punchline

Suppression everywhere.
Preservation only when bound.

The triplet binds. The binding repairs the target–distractor margin. But how? Two channels could carry the repair: push the distractor's logit down, or hold the target's logit up. Or both. Two controls separate them.

Swap: replace only the target activation with a donor item's, keeping subject and query matched. Source-position perturbation present, binding broken on one position. Random: patch all three positions, but from a completely mismatched item. Full perturbation, no binding match.

DISSOCIATION at rest
1 Red bars fall everywhere — distractor suppression is universal.
2 Green bars collapse in Swap and Random, but hold in Matched.
3 Δm — the margin signature — is positive only for Matched.

Distractor suppression is what any patch can do. Target preservation is what binding can do. The matched-binding signature isn't in pushing the wrong answer down — that happens with any perturbation. It's in holding the right answer up.

08  ·  What it means

Override is a partial competition.

When a model is told doctor means forest, it doesn't unlearn that doctor lives in a hospital. It learns to make forest win the local contest. But hospital's representation is still running underneath — same lexical-prior channel, same target–distractor axis — leaking through in proportion to its strength.

For systems that have to respect document-specific definitions — glossaries, RAG, technical doc QA, system prompts — that's a real distinction. A model that looks like it overrode the prior may still be biased toward it underneath. The Stroop card gives you a per-item measurement; the dissociation tells you which logit channel signals true binding versus mere perturbation.
